Step 2: How Does It Make You Feel?

Step 3: Does It Feel Exaggerated?

Look for clues. Does it sound too dramatic? News that uses extreme language can often be "clickbait" or fake. Why they are exaggerating? Sounds a little bit fake, doesn't it?. What do you think about this one?

Step 4: Is There a Clear Message?

Does the news make you want something? Maybe a product or lifestyle? Be cautious—news that plays on our desires might be more of an ad than true information.

Step 1: Feeling Suspicious? Let’s Talk to Others!

In the last step, you asked yourself questions about this news story: does it feel exaggerated? Does it make you feel a certain way? Now, let’s go deeper by checking with our peers—our friends, family, and teachers. Instead of simply asking them, “Do you think this is true or false?” let’s ask them about their reactions. What does their intuition tell them? Does it feel trustworthy or a little off?

Sometimes, one of your friends, family members, or teachers might be an expert on the topic and can even suggest sources to check. Talking with others can help you see if the feelings and questions you had are shared by others, or if they can offer new insights. By comparing these reactions, you’re not just asking if the news is true or false—you’re building a clearer view of the story together. This conversation with people around you is like a first line of fact-checking, helping you to identify sources or experts who might confirm or clarify the story.

Step 2: Understanding Reliable Sources

What makes a source reliable? Imagine you’re building a tower. If you only use flimsy pieces, the tower will fall. But if you use strong, steady pieces, your tower will be solid. Information works the same way. A reliable source is built on the shoulders of others, using strong foundations—facts, studies, and knowledge that others have confirmed. This is often called “standing on the shoulders of giants,” meaning that when people write news, articles, or stories, they rely on trusted information that came before them.

For example, at universities, people who write articles submit them to a group of peers, or experts, who review and discuss them. They also build on a “library” of sources—reputable studies and references. This process helps make sure the information is accurate and trustworthy.

Let’s practice this with the story of Little Red Riding Hood.

If we wanted to verify details about her story, like who wrote it and what color her hood is, we could look at sources that reference the original version by the Brothers Grimm. Here are some reliable sources you could use to check facts about stories or information you find:

  • SRF (Swiss Radio and Television) - Contains educational and factual programs, sometimes covering classic stories like Little Red Riding Hood.
  • Youth Media Research Sites:
    • Jugend und Medien - Resources on learning to research for young readers.
    • Klexikon - A safe, kid-friendly encyclopedia.
    • FragFinn - A search engine for children.
    • Blinde Kuh - A safe search site for children.
    • Helles Köpfchen - Information and research platform for children.
  • International Kid-Friendly Search Engines:
    • Kiddle - Safe visual search engine for kids (English).
    • KidsSearch - Another child-friendly search engine (English).
  • Google Scholar - Provides access to reviewed studies and articles for more in-depth research.
  • Wikipedia (German) - General knowledge resource; best to double-check with additional sources.
  • Duda.news - A news platform for young readers.

By understanding what makes a source reliable, you’ll start to recognize trustworthy information and see when something is just “pretending” to be true. Now that you’re ready, let’s check the sources and see if we can confirm the details of Little Red Riding Hood’s story—and whether her hood is red or, surprisingly, blue!

"Once Upon a Time… Or Is It Real?"

Have you ever heard stories about Santa Claus, the Easter Bunny, or Little Red Riding Hood? These are tales that start with “Once upon a time…” and take us to a world of imagination and wonder. When we hear that line, we know we’re entering a pretend world where anything can happen. We don’t expect it to be real, but we enjoy believing in it for a moment, like a fun game.

“Once upon a time” is a signal that helps us know the story is make-believe. It’s a way for storytellers to let us know, “This isn’t true, but it’s going to be fun!”

But in the world we live in today, not all stories are this clear. We scroll through social media and see many “stories” that don’t start with “Once upon a time.” Some might look like real news, but how can we know if they’re true? Unlike fairy tales, fake news doesn’t come with a sign telling us it’s pretend.

So, how can we tell the difference? That’s where our next skill comes in: checking sources. By looking for trusted sources, we can find out if a story is just fun fiction, like Little Red Riding Hood (or, is she actually wearing a blue hood now?), or if it’s based on real facts. Let’s dive in and start learning how to find the truth behind the story!
